Least-squares—a tourist's guide

Before you travel, it is often good to know what to expect—how to get there and where to go (and why). Along those lines, this is a guide to the essential parts of the least squares (LS) method. So, if you decide to visit, your trip will be smoother and better informed. (Before we begin, please note that footnotes (e.g., ) are used extensively.)

LS fits a model to data . To be concrete, consider an audio amplifier. An ideal amplifier is a multiplier; it makes the output (\(y\)) a certain multiple (\(k_1\), say) greater than the input (\(x\)). However, in practice, the amplifier will distort as the volume is increased, and a better model is

$$ y = k_1 x + k_3 x^3 $$

    Note that linearity in the parameters is different from linearity in \(x\)...

    If our model was \(y = k_1x + \ln(k_3)x^3 \), then the model would not be linear in its parameters (doubling (or tripling) \(k_3\) would not make the contribution from the second term two (or three) times greater).

    Least squares, with a model that is linear in its parameters, is known as "linear least squares"—generally, if someone just says "least squares", they mean "linear least squares".
